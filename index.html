ITC Limited – Procurement Optimization in Chilli Supply Chain

During my internship with ITC's Agri Business Division (Spices), I was responsible for executing end-to-end procurement operations across key chilli-growing clusters in Vilathikulam and Pudur, Thoothukudi district.

I executed and managed procurement of ~130 MT of dry chilli (including ~100 MT of Gundu/white chilli variants and ~30 MT of Samba chilli) from a sampled pool of ~130 MT, translating to a total procurement value of ~₹76 lakh. The sourcing process involved field-level engagement with farmers, quality sampling, lab-based validation (Guntur), and final lot selection aligned with export-grade standards.

A key operational challenge was high rejection rates due to stringent quality benchmarks, coupled with price volatility driven by Guntur market trends and fragmented farmer networks. To address this, I coordinated closely with local intermediaries (wholesale aggregators) to streamline sourcing and improve lot accessibility. Additionally, I optimized sample logistics by shifting from conventional transport to faster, cost-efficient bus-based dispatch, reducing turnaround time for quality validation and accelerating procurement cycles.

Operating under a market-linked procurement strategy, I ensured alignment with prevailing price benchmarks while maintaining strict quality compliance. Through structured sampling, Excel-based tracking of lots and quantities, and on-ground coordination, I successfully delivered procurement within ~40 days, improving procurement cycle efficiency by an estimated ~25–30% compared to traditional sampling and logistics timelines, while ensuring timely aggregation of high-quality produce for downstream processing.

This experience strengthened my ability to manage complex agri-supply chains, balance quality vs. speed trade-offs, and execute large-scale procurement under uncertain market conditions, contributing to more efficient and reliable sourcing operations.

Dry Chilli Price Forecasting & Procurement Optimisation

Unpredictable price swings in India's dry chilli market expose commodity buyers to margin risk and force farmers into distress sales. A 20-year analysis of price behaviour revealed a consistent seasonal pattern — enabling buyers to time procurement and lock in cost advantages before peak season.

20 Yrs
Price Data Modelled
Problem

Buyers overpay when purchasing at price peaks; farmers undersell during glut periods. Tamil Nadu's price instability is 5× the national average — making the market structurally difficult to navigate without data.

Approach
  • 20 years of AGMARKNET market price data (2004–2024)
  • Instability benchmarking: Tamil Nadu vs national
  • Seasonal price index built month-by-month
  • Forecasted price trajectory through 2025 using best-fit time-series model
Key Insights
  • Prices peak every December — pre-harvest tightness is structural, not random
  • Jan–Mar is the optimal procurement window (lowest seasonal index)
  • Prices forecast to rise through late 2025
  • TN instability 5× national — requires localised risk management

Dec↑ Annual price peak

Recommendations
  • Buy Jan–Mar — prices are at seasonal floor, 15–20% below December peak
  • Build cold-chain buffer stock for Oct–Dec release to capture price upside
  • Set price-trigger contracts with farmers to lock in floor prices
  • Insights aligned with procurement practices observed during internship at ITC Guntur — an early-sampling protocol was in place that significantly reduced post-acceptance rejections during the implementation phase
💡
Why This Matters
India processes over 1.9 million tonnes of dry chilli annually. A procurement timing shift of just 2–3 months — guided by seasonal price intelligence — has the potential to meaningfully reduce input costs for buyers. This kind of data-driven procurement planning is directly replicable across seasonal commodity desks in India's agri-FMCG sector.
Business Impact:  Seasonal price insights relevant to procurement timing at ITC · Early-sampling protocol contributed to reducing rejection rates · Price forecast under journal review (2025)
Key Takeaway Contract farming delivers a ~30% income premium for smallholder farmers — but only when structured with a floor-price guarantee plus spot-market upside. Risk perception, not economics, is the primary barrier to scale.

Contract vs. Non-Contract Farming: Income Impact & Scalability

Farmer income instability creates procurement risk for buyers and poverty traps for producers. A 120-farmer comparative study quantified the income gap between contracted and uncontracted farmers and identified the design features of a scalable contract model.

+30%
Income Premium Identified
Problem

Smallholder farmers outside formal contracts face price uncertainty, input access gaps, and lower yields — creating a structural income disadvantage that feeds both farmer distress and supply volatility for commodity buyers.

Approach
  • Primary survey of 120 farmers (contract vs non-contract groups)
  • Propensity Score Matching to eliminate selection bias
  • Regression to isolate the true income impact of contracting
  • Yield and price-realisation benchmarking across both groups
Key Insights
  • Contract farmers earn ~30% more income on average
  • Better price realisation and higher yield in contract group
  • Input access and technical support are the key differentiators
  • Risk perception — not economics — is the main adoption barrier

India Buffalo Meat Export Strategy: Market Stability & Growth Recovery

India, the world's #1 buffalo meat exporter, saw exports decline at −1.9% CAGR post-2014 despite a long-term structural advantage. A 30-year study identified which markets to defend, which to exit, and where to invest to reclaim lost ground by 2030.

$3.7B
Sector Analysed
Problem

A sector contributing 92.1% of India's animal product export value entered a decade-long decline due to regulatory fragmentation, quality compliance gaps, and rising competition from Brazil and Australia — while the underlying market demand remained strong.

Approach
  • 30 years of APEDA export data (1994–2024) across 11 countries
  • Market loyalty scored via Markov chain transition matrix
  • Competitive position measured using Revealed Comparative Advantage
  • Policy shift timing identified via structural break tests
  • Production hotspots mapped across 7 states using ArcGIS
Key Insights
  • Vietnam (73%) & Malaysia (75%) are the most loyal — defend and deepen
  • Iraq retains only 5.8% — actively losing market, low strategic value
  • India's competitive advantage (RCA >1) is intact despite the decline
  • UP alone produces 21.5% of output — infrastructure investment here has outsized return

1.745M MT forecast 2030

Recommendations
  • Double down on Vietnam & Malaysia — highest retention, lowest risk
  • Enter Australia & South America — high meat consumption, near-zero Indian market share
  • Invest in abattoir upgrades in UP, Punjab, Maharashtra — these 3 states = 38% of output
  • Harmonise state-level slaughter regulations to eliminate inter-state supply friction
  • Scale male calf salvaging programs — largest untapped supply lever
💡
Why This Matters
India controls 54% of the world's buffalo population and holds a meaningful cost and quality advantage over Brazil and Australia. The data suggests the decline is driven by regulatory and operational factors rather than a structural loss of competitiveness. Focusing on supply chain compliance and high-retention markets — rather than expanding production — emerges as the more tractable growth lever. The 2030 forecast of 1.745M MT reflects what is achievable under the right policy and infrastructure conditions.
Business Impact:  Forecast: 1.745M MT by 2030 · Published ICAR 2025 (DOI: 10.56093/ijans.v95i5.166312) · Offers policy-relevant insights for export strategy
